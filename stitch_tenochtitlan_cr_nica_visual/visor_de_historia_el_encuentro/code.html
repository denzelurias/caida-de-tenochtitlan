November 8, 1519

The Meeting on the Causeway

A collision of two worlds on the great thoroughfare of Iztapalapa, fundamentally altering the trajectory of global history.

The encounter between Motecuhzoma Xocoyotzin and Hernán Cortés occurred on the southern causeway leading into the island city of Tenochtitlan. According to both indigenous accounts preserved in the Florentine Codex and Spanish chronicles like those of Bernal Díaz del Castillo, the event was orchestrated with immense diplomatic protocol and underlying tension.

Motecuhzoma, carried on a litter adorned with quetzal feathers, gold, silver, and pearls, descended to meet the Spaniards. The ground before him was swept clean, and mantles were laid down so his sandaled feet would not touch the earth—a mark of his quasi-divine status. Cortés, dismounting his horse, approached with his captains. Through the translation of Doña Marina (La Malinche) and Jerónimo de Aguilar, the first verbal exchanges were mediated, fraught with linguistic and cultural misunderstandings that would later be heavily debated by historians.

"Our lord, you are weary. The journey has tired you, but now you have arrived on the earth. You have come to your city, Mexico. You have come here to sit on your throne..."
— Nahuatl greeting attributed to Motecuhzoma, Florentine Codex

The interpretation of this speech remains one of the most contested aspects of the conquest narrative. While early Spanish chroniclers interpreted it as an act of submission and the surrender of the empire to Charles V, modern ethnohistorians recognize it as standard Nahua rhetorical courtesy (huehuetlatolli), expressing polite deference rather than political capitulation.

translate The Role of Translation

Communication relied on a complex chain: Cortés spoke Spanish to Aguilar, who translated to Chontal Maya for Malintzin, who then translated to Nahuatl for Motecuhzoma. The nuance lost in this process profoundly shaped the historical record.
